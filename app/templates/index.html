Welcome to Why Cat

Why Cat is a simple checklist that helps consumers decide whether a website with online health information is trustworthy. A trustworthy website has good quality online health information.

The checklist has 12 questions.

Questions 1 - 11 each focus on one detail that can help you judge whether a website with online health information is trustworthy. Each question will ask you to give a website a score. Each question has extra information and a link to an example of a trustworthy website.

Question 12 asks you to decide whether a website with online health information is trustworthy. Your answers to Questions 1 to 11 will help you answer this question. You should only decide that a website as trustworthy if you give it good scores for Questions 1 to 11

There is also a tutorial, which shows how we used the checklist to decide whether websites with online health information were trustworthy.

About Us

Why Cat has been developed by university academics, a doctor and computer science students.

Dr Karen Scott is a senior lecturer and education academic who specialises in medical education, elearning, and health education research. Dr Scott works for the Discipline of Child and Adolescent Health at The University of Sydney and is based at the Children’s Hospital at Westmead, Sydney, Australia.

Associate Professor Patrina Caldwell is a senior staff specialist paediatrician who develops eHealth and conducts medical and health education research. A/Prof Caldwell works for the Children’s Hospital at Westmead, Sydney and the Discipline of Child and Adolescent Health at The University of Sydney, Australia.

Macquarie University Department of Computer Science students developed Why Cat as part of the Professional and Community Engagement Program: Ali Akturk, Christopher Chan, Laura Huang, Francene Peaston, and Travis Wormald.

Disclaimer

Why Cat aims to help consumers assess the trustworthiness of online health information. It is not intended to replace medical advice and any questions regarding a medical diagnosis or treatment should be directed to a medical practitioner. Why Cat was developed without any commercial funding and is not influenced by any commercial organisations.
